Q1. What's the meaning of EK-DRD?

Drug Repositioning (also known as drug repurposing, re-profiling, re-tasking or therapeutic switching) is the application of known drugs and compounds to treat new indications (i.e., new diseases).
As shown in the above figure, docetaxel is a FDA-approved marketed drug for cancer treatment. Through searching our EK-DRD database, you can find new efficacy of docetaxel for the treatment of malaria. This can provide researchers with crucial information about the new efficacy of docetaxel.
Expeimental Knowledge-Based Drug Repositioning Database (EK-DRD) is a comprehensive, freely accessible, online database which provides a versatile resource for the display, search, and analysis of structure, function, pathway, disease, network, and related well-established drug repositioning annotation for 1963 FDA approved drugs (including 102 withdraw drugs), data in EK-DRD is annually updated and freely served for biologists and medicinal chemists interested in drug repositioning for drug discovery

Q5. How to define repositioning drugs? In target, cell, organism and clinical trial level?

The data used in EK-DRD have as the main components information on drugs with FDA approval and experimental information on drug repositioning at four levels (target, cell, organism, and clinical trial). In general, FDA-approved drugs have their indications (specific diseases) and targets. In this study, the repositioning target for a given drug refers to drug target other than FDA-approved target. This definition can be extended to the repositioning data from cell, organism and clinical trial experiment. Therefore, drugs with information in those four repositioning levels can be defined as repositioning drugs.





Q6. How many drugs in these 8889 clinical trials by covering how many diseases?

Approximately 293 diseases involved in these 8889 clinical trials according to classification of diseases in ICD-10 (version 2016, https://icd.who.int/browse10/2016/en#/), including general categories and subcategories.
